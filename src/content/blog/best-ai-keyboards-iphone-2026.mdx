## What makes an AI keyboard actually good?

After testing every iOS AI keyboard for the last 6 months, four things separate the good from the gimmicky:

1. **It runs inside your keyboard.** Not as a separate app. Not as a Safari extension. Real, in-keyboard. Otherwise it's just a fancy bookmark to ChatGPT.
2. **It does more than fix grammar.** Grammar is table stakes — the upgrade is changing tone, translating, asking AI a question, and generating replies, all without leaving Messages.
3. **It respects your privacy.** Text should only leave your phone when you tap a feature — never silently logged. Apple auto-hides keyboards on password fields; your keyboard should never override that.
4. **It supports your language.** "AI keyboard" doesn't mean much if it only autocompletes in English.

## 2. Apple Intelligence Writing Tools

Apple's native Writing Tools (introduced in iOS 18, expanded in 2026) are genuinely impressive — rewrite, proofread, summarize, and tone shift, baked into iOS. They're free.

The catch: they only work on **iPhone 15 Pro and newer** (and select iPads). If you're on an iPhone 14 or older, you literally cannot use them. They also don't translate, which is a huge omission for anyone with multilingual contacts.

If you have a brand-new iPhone and you only need English rewriting, this might be all you need. For everyone else, keep reading.

## 3. Grammarly Keyboard

Grammarly's been the default "smart keyboard" recommendation for years. In 2026 it's still excellent at one specific thing — catching grammar and spelling mistakes as you type — and it's free for the basic version.

But "smart keyboard" doesn't really apply anymore. Grammarly doesn't do tone change, translation, or AI replies. The premium version ($5.83/month annual) adds tone suggestions but in English only.

If you write a lot in English and you only care about typo correction, Grammarly's fine. For anything else in 2026, it feels like a one-trick pony.

## 4. TypeAI

TypeAI takes a privacy-first angle: most processing happens on-device, with optional cloud features. It ships ~6 AI tools, supports ~15 languages, and is priced similarly to KeyAI ($5.99/wk · $39.99/yr).

It's a solid choice if you write primarily in English and you're nervous about cloud AI. The trade-off is fewer features and tighter language support.

## 5. Keybot

Keybot is the budget option — fewer tools, fewer languages, but a familiar layout. At $6.99/week it's actually more expensive weekly than KeyAI, with worse yearly value ($49.99 vs $39.99). It's a reasonable starting point if you're new to AI keyboards and don't write in many languages.
